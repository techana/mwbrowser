The MSX Computer Story

A short history of a well-loved 8-bit standard

In the mid 1980s a loose group of Japanese manufacturers rallied around a single shared hardware specification for home computers. The platform was called MSX and its goal was simple: a machine from one brand should run software from any other. For the first time, buyers could walk into a shop, pick up a cartridge, and be sure it would work regardless of the badge on the keyboard.


The first MSX machines arrived in 1983 and were joined by models from Sony, Panasonic, Sanyo, Toshiba, Yamaha, Philips, and a dozen smaller houses. Every one carried a 3.58 MHz Z80A, 16 or 32 KB of RAM, Microsoft BASIC in ROM, and a TI video chip with 16 colours on screen. The differences lived at the margins.

MSX2 and Beyond

By 1985 the standard was refreshed. MSX2 kept the Z80 at its heart but paired it with a much stronger video chip, the Yamaha V9938. Screens ran at 512 by 212 in 16 colours or 256 by 212 in 256 colours. Main RAM grew to 64 KB and video RAM to 128 KB. A real-time clock was added and a disk drive became standard.

Games from this era pushed the hardware hard. Konami, Compile, and Hudson wrote scrollers and platformers that felt closer to arcade boards than to typical home fare. Series like Metal Gear, Parodius, Nemesis, and Aleste were born here.

MSX2+ arrived in 1988 with extra video modes, natural-scrolling hardware, and a YM2413 sound chip built in. It was a Japan-only refinement, but the machines are still prized by collectors for their smooth scrolling and rich sound.

MSX Turbo R

The final generation, the MSX Turbo R, launched in 1990 from Panasonic alone. It added an R800 processor alongside the Z80A, delivering roughly four times the integer performance. The R800 kept binary compatibility with the Z80 so existing code ran faster. The top model shipped with a full megabyte of RAM, built-in MIDI, and PCM sampling.

The early 1990s market had shifted to MS-DOS clones and the Amiga. Only two Panasonic models shipped, and production wound down over a couple of years.

Legacy

Despite officially ending, MSX never disappeared. A loyal community in Japan, the Netherlands, Spain, Brazil, and the Gulf states kept writing software through the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s. Flash carts, FPGA clones, and current-day hardware expansions keep the platform alive.

Annual meetings still take place in Tilburg, Barcelona, and Tokyo. Emulators are mature, every BIOS ROM is preserved, and the scene has genuine continuity.
