The MSX Story

In the early 1980s the home computer market was a battlefield of incompatible machines. The Commodore 64, ZX Spectrum, Apple II and dozens of Japanese designs all shipped with mutually unintelligible BASIC dialects, different graphics chips and proprietary disk formats. Software written for one machine refused to run on any other. MSX was conceived to end this chaos.

1. The Birth of a Standard

On the 27th of June 1983, ASCII Corporation in Japan and Microsoft jointly announced a hardware specification under the brand MSX. The acronym was never officially explained -- "Machines with Software eXchangeability" is the most repeated retro-fit -- but its meaning was plain: any computer wearing the MSX badge would run any software released for any other MSX, regardless of who built it.

The architect was Kazuhiko (Kay) Nishi, co-founder of ASCII and head of Microsoft's Far East division. Nishi modelled MSX on the VHS tape format: a strict hardware contract licensed to many manufacturers who would then compete on case design, peripherals and price, but never on raw architecture. It was a bold answer to the question that defined the decade -- "why do all these computers refuse to talk to each other?"

Microsoft supplied a localised version of its 8-bit BASIC, already battle-tested on the TRS-80 and Apple II, and ASCII drew up the slot-and-cartridge bus that became the MSX expansion standard. Within a year nearly every major Japanese consumer- electronics firm had a machine on shop shelves.

2. The MSX1 Hardware

The original 1983 specification reads like a tour of the most popular off-the-shelf chips of the era:

  • CPU: Zilog Z80A running at 3.58 MHz
  • Video: Texas Instruments TMS9918A, 256x192 pixels, 16 colours, 32 hardware sprites
  • Sound: General Instrument AY-3-8910 PSG, three square- wave voices plus noise
  • RAM: from 8 KB up to 64 KB, depending on the model
  • ROM: 32 KB containing Microsoft Extended BASIC and the BIOS
  • Two cartridge slots and a 50-pin expansion bus
  • Cassette port at 1200 or 2400 baud, plus optional floppy

The TMS9918A had already powered the ColecoVision and the Texas Instruments TI-99/4A, so a vast library of arcade techniques transferred to MSX overnight. The Z80A and the AY-3-8910 were the same chips that drove countless arcade boards, which is why MSX ports of Konami and Capcom titles felt astonishingly close to their coin-op originals.

3. The Manufacturers

The licensing model worked. By 1985 the MSX logo appeared on machines from:

  • Sony -- HitBit series, including the legendary HB-F1XD
  • Panasonic (then Matsushita) -- the FS-A1 line and the later Turbo R flagships
  • Sanyo, Toshiba, Mitsubishi, Hitachi, Fujitsu, Pioneer, JVC, Canon
  • Yamaha -- the music-oriented CX5M with built-in FM synth
  • Philips -- VG-8010 and VG-8020 for the European market
  • Spectravideo -- the SVI-728, born of the prototype that inspired the standard in the first place
  • Daewoo, Goldstar, Samsung -- the Korean school-computer market
  • Sakhr / Al Alamiah -- the entire Arabic-speaking world

At its peak, more than thirty companies in seven countries shipped MSX-compatible hardware. No single 8-bit platform before or since has commanded that breadth of industry support.

4. MSX2: A Generation Leap (1985)

By the autumn of 1985 the original MSX hardware was beginning to look anaemic next to 16-bit newcomers like the Atari ST and the Commodore Amiga. ASCII responded with the MSX2 standard, keeping the Z80A but replacing nearly everything else:

  • Yamaha V9938 VDP, up to 512x212 with 256 colours from a 512-colour palette
  • 128 KB of video RAM (rising to 192 KB on later models)
  • Hardware vertical scrolling and a blitter for fast copies
  • Real-time clock with non-volatile memory for settings
  • MSX-DOS 2 with sub-directories, FAT12 disks and a CP/M- compatible API

For the first time, MSX machines could display photographic images. Konami's Vampire Killer and King's Valley II showcased smooth scrolling worlds that simply could not exist on MSX1. The 8-bit format had bought itself another six years of life.

5. MSX2+ and MSX Turbo R

The 1988 MSX2+ revision added the V9958 VDP, capable of horizontal scroll and two new "natural" screen modes (Screen 10 and Screen 12) with 12 499 or 19 268 simultaneous colours -- a remarkable feat for a Z80-driven machine. Most MSX2+ models also shipped with the Yamaha YM2413 OPLL, the same nine-voice FM synthesiser found in the Sega Mark III.

In 1990 Panasonic delivered the final and most exotic step, the MSX Turbo R. Its FS-A1ST and FS-A1GT were the only machines ever built around the ASCII R800 CPU -- an internally redesigned Z80 that retired most of its instructions to single-clock execution, multiplying performance by roughly four. The Turbo R kept the original Z80A on the board for backward compatibility and switched between the two at software request.

Sadly, no other manufacturer joined the Turbo R generation. The 16-bit wave had carried the rest of the market away, and MSX retreated to its strongholds in Japan, the Netherlands, Spain, Brazil and the Arab world.

6. The Software Library

MSX inherited the cream of the 8-bit arcade tradition. Konami became the platform's de-facto champion, producing more than a hundred cartridges, many of them using their custom SCC chip -- a five-voice waveform synthesiser hidden inside the game ROM. Tracks from Salamander, Gradius 2 and Space Manbow remain instantly recognisable to anyone who grew up with the format.

In 1987 a young Hideo Kojima released his first major game on the Konami MSX2: Metal Gear. The stealth genre was born on this 8-bit platform, four years before the Sony PlayStation existed. Its sequel, Metal Gear 2: Solid Snake, refined the formula in 1990 and is still cited as the direct ancestor of every modern stealth title.

Other major contributors were:

  • Compile -- the Aleste shooters, Golvellius
  • Hudson Soft -- Bomberman, Bomber King
  • Falcom -- the Ys and Xanadu RPGs
  • T&E Soft -- Hydlide, Daiva
  • ASCII -- the bundled BASIC, plus the Randar adventure series

7. في العالم العربي

خارج اليابان، لم تتبنّ أي منطقة معيار MSX كما تبنّاه العالم العربي. قامت الشركة الكويتية العالمية، تحت العلامة التجارية صخر، بترخيص مواصفة MSX عام 1984 وبَنَت حولها منظومة حاسوبية متكاملة.

شُحنت أجهزة صخر AX-150 و AX-170 و AX-330 و AX-350 و AX-370 ببيوس مخصص يحوي مجموعة محارف ثنائية اللغة: رموز ASCII القياسية في المواقع 0x20 إلى 0x7E، وأبجدية عربية كاملة وفق ISO-8859-6 فوق 0xA0 تتضمن الأشكال الموضعية الأربعة لكل حرف، إضافة إلى الأرقام الهندية في المواقع 0xB0 إلى 0xB9. مفتاح في لوحة المفاتيح يبدّل بين الإدخال اللاتيني والعربي فوراً.

خلال أواخر الثمانينات، أصبحت سلسلة AX الحاسوب التعليمي المعتمد في المدارس عبر المملكة العربية السعودية والكويت والإمارات العربية المتحدة ومصر. كتب جيل كامل من المبرمجين العرب أول سطر بيسك على جهاز صخر MSX، ولا يزال كثير منهم يحتفظ بواحد منها حتى اليوم.

جلب جهاز AX-370 على وجه الخصوص ميزات MSX2 (شريحة V9938، و128 كيلوبايت من ذاكرة الفيديو، ونظام MSX-DOS) إلى السوق العربية. وهو العتاد المستهدف لعارض HTML الذي تقرأ به هذا المقال: متصفح صغير أصيل يقرأ النصوص العربية بترميز ISO-8859-6 مباشرة من مولّد المحارف في البيوس، ويعكس التخطيط للكتل المكتوبة من اليمين إلى اليسار، ويدعم الصور المدمجة بصيغة SC6 مثل شعار صخر أعلاه.

8. Decline and Legacy

By 1992 Microsoft had moved its consumer attention to Windows, and the consumer-electronics giants that backed MSX had pivoted to the Super Famicom, the Mega Drive and the emerging PC clone market. Panasonic stopped Turbo R production in 1995. Officially, the MSX standard was dead.

In practice it never quite died. The MSX user clubs in Tilburg, Barcelona and São Paulo continued to host yearly conventions throughout the 1990s. New game cartridges appeared from independent developers under the MSXdev competition (running annually since 2003), and ASCII's successors released the 1chipMSX FPGA reimplementation in 2006 -- a single Altera Cyclone II that runs every MSX, MSX2, MSX2+ and Turbo R title at original speed.

Today the platform thrives in three forms:

  • Original hardware -- still common on the secondhand market, especially in Japan and the Netherlands.
  • FPGA recreations -- the 1chipMSX, the MSXVR, the Zemmix Neo, the OMEGA, the SX-2 and others.
  • Emulators -- openMSX, blueMSX and fMSX, each preserving thousands of games and applications byte-for-byte.

9. Conclusion

MSX never dominated the world the way VHS did, but its licensing dream proved correct. A single binary cartridge inserted into a Sony HitBit, a Philips VG-8020 or a Sakhr AX-370 will produce, within milliseconds, exactly the same output. Forty years after Kay Nishi's announcement, the machines still talk to each other.

The story of MSX is the story of cooperation in a market that rewards lock-in -- a quiet, persistent reminder that common standards can outlast every fashion that tried to replace them.
